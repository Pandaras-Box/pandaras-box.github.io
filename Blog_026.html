Romance in games? Nah. Let’s talk relationships between players in video games instead

Even if it’s tenuous at best, with today being Valentine’s Day, it would be rude to not find some possibility of linking our weekly nonsense to this, the most commercialized of yearly debacles. While romance in games is certainly an interesting topic, it’s not really one I feel I could present a fresh take on at present, so I thought I’d instead approach my initially planned discussion on co-op in video games from a themed angle instead.

When it comes to designing and developing games, often the bulk of the parts in development aren’t individually going to be all that maddening (at least once you’ve settled on which mechanics you want to include), and designing worlds, stories and levels for single player experiences follow a similar path of being difficult to refine, certainly, but getting a rough version of your creative vision is usually more a case of time spent working than anything else. With multiplayer and cooperative experiences however, it becomes much trickier to create any form of lasting, meaningful engagement from players for many reasons. One of the most difficult considerations is that of the random element unique to these types of games and game modes, that of the relationships formed (both brief and lasting) between players.

“Emergent narrative” is a term that’s been bandied about quite a lot recently, especially when it comes to multiplayer games development. The hope seems to be that providing the players with the right combination of tools and mechanics will cover the cracks on any lacking areas within the pre-built narrative created by the team. This isn’t necessarily a bad thing - far from it, in fact - as it can often lead to some of the most uniquely wonderful experiences in games, or just some good humour from players (case in point:
).

However, the emergent narrative in multiplayer and co-op in many cases relies heavily on the pre-existing relationships, often those outside of the games themselves, that the players have with one another… which, should players not be in particularly imaginative moods, or just tired for example, can lead to a more lackluster experience.

I’d like to go into a broader discussion (maybe next week) on how games craft these connections successfully (or otherwise) with particular focus on mine (and Dicky’s) recent experiences with a few co-op games. In particular I hope to look at those experiences in which those provided tools to create unique and fun experiences upon repeated plays with different people, ended up feeling far too quickly as if the well was drying up and we’d seen all there was far too soon!
