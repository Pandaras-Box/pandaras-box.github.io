With tabletop games over the past couple years, we have found that playing a variety of both card and board games has led to the team being much more enthused at work, has helped to improving logical thinking across the board, an understanding of how to both work to and subvert expectations, and has also provided encouragement for longer focus in the work day with the incentive of a relaxing session of intrigue and backstabbing lined up for the end-of-day. We genuinely think this has been incredibly beneficial (and not just because we have more fun in the workplace!), and the recent addition to the office, Coup: Rebellion G54 (or G54, for short), the card game David was found finishing sleeving this morning, has immediately begun to highlight many of these benefits.

Context first, of course. The base of Coup is a card game (to which G54 is a ridiculously expansive sequel) and a game of deception and strategy, with each player drawing from a pool of 5 different active roles (there’s 3 cards of each role) with differing abilities, including superior money gain, information gathering or the power to just plain take others out of the game. The deception comes in the form of players performing actions regardless of whether they possess a copy of the role that has that ability in their hand. If opponents believe they are lying, though, their bluff can be called and the player in the wrong loses a life (of which they have two). The interactions between the various roles are where the nuance lies, with some blocking others actions and so forth. While Coup contains 5 roles, with up to four new replacement roles being added by expansions and promos, G54 contains a whopping 25(!) by default, with another 6 being added by the expansion to that.
In the base game of Coup, and in G54, characters fall into 4 categories: Finance, Force, Communications and Special, with 2 from the latter and 1 from each of the others in each game. The interactions between these roles provide a certain amount of inspiration when it comes to considering the interactions between the various pillars of gameplay within a given project. The additional variety of characters in the recently acquired expansion has been especially interesting in suggesting how the variety of stats and abilities in Leitmotif should be balanced with regards to each other - as an analogy to Coup, if a Force role is too strong, it can make a Finance role too weak, because they’ll just die before they can use their wealth, or it can make a Communications role too strong as well, because then they can just dig for the overpowered role. While this is particularly relevant to our current project, seeing as an RPG features numerous interactions of the sort Coup thrives upon, it is of course only one, albeit excellent, example of the usage that tabletop games can be put to when it comes to digital games design.

With other tabletop games that have found themselves in front of our team, aspects such as co-operative play, the use of RNG (random number generated) elements, difficulty curves/accessibility and depth of play have been wonderfully highlighted, leading to otherwise tough decisions in our projects’ development becoming much easier (or the reverse, should a gaping flaw in our original choices be brought to light!), all the while keeping our spirits high, especially during the more monotonous periods of development.

I could go into much more nuance on the particulars of each of these, but it might be more fun if I simply make some recommendations for you all to try if you haven’t already.
